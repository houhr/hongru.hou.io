Next Muni is a bus arrival time prediction app for people using the Muni system daily. Based on location and search history, the app automatically shows desired scheduled information when opened. Apple Watch sync functionality provides users with the confidence of accurate, updated Muni schedules on the go.

The Problem

Competitive Products Analysis

I initiated this app based on my own needs. As a student who lives in San Francisco and commutes by taking Muni buses everyday, I have tried several different bus arrival time prediction apps. However, none of these apps are smart and have enough customized information for individuals. Therefore, people need spend tons of time to interact with the app and find the right information they need.

The following table shows how many interactions are needed for a single tasks in two mainstream Muni apps and Google Maps.

Design Goal

The ultimate goal is to show people the correct information right after they open the app in their follow up same tasks usage. By grouping and saving the data of where and when people used the app and which Muni routes they searched, we can reduce the time and effort that people needed by guessing their needs base on their search history and location.

By doing this project, I would like to simplify the connection between people and the right information they need. So, they could live a more efficient life.

Design Process

User Journey User Flow Paper Sketch Mood Board

The targeted users of bus arrival time prediction apps are people who have a relatively fixed daily routine — go back and forth between home and school or company — and take public transit every weekday. They know which bus they should take and where the station is, or they can get this information from other prepositive apps like Google Maps. However, they don't want to open Google Maps and input destination everytime when they need the time information.

It's also important to go through the whole process in the shoes of users new to the app, and try to understand where, when, why and how they use this kind of apps. After having a clear idea of what the interaction logic should be, then I started to sketch some rough ideas on the paper. A mood board with all the themes I wanted to communicate is also necessary to bring the whole idea to life. I even customized the style of maps based on the color palette that generated from the mood board.

Iterative Design

Test Early and Often Self-criticism Ask for Feedback

The first version of the design could finish the main part of the job successfully, and the most important thing was that users could find the right information they need in follow up usages (same tasks) — it's only one tap away!

However, after I tested it with my classmates, it turned out that some of them had some doubts about the app — what does the time mean? And I also got some feedback from a Facebook's HR, he suggested me to think about what the app looks like when users just downloaded it.

Moreover, after I did something else and came back to look at this version with fresh eyes, I also got some my own idea about how can I improve the app. So, I started to tweak the app and then the second version came out.

I made multiple improvement in version 2:

  1. Designed a onboarding screen for new users. Since they haven't search for any routes yet, showing them some nearby routes may help them get started and keep efficient. And a brief discription of how this app works can also help them get familiar with the app and make the onboarding process more smooth and friendly.

  2. Be inspired by the concept of "nearby routes", I added it to search page right below the search bar. Because of the cost (no matter time or effort) of inputs is much bigger than taps, it could save tons of time if the user could find the targeted routs in the list.

  3. The contextual user guide also appear when users finished their first "search" or "save" operation, so they know how to edit the item in the list.

  4. Redesigned the clock down icon. So, now people can tell the meaning behind the time correctly.

Prototyping

High-fidelity Prototype

Until now, I have only finished half of the design work (maybe I should say β version, I know digital products are never finished). There's another half that I would like to finish with code — transition and animation.

Although I can get a low-fidelity prototype much quicker in Keynote or inVision, code could give me the most precise control. Now that transition and animation are all about feelings, there's no reason not to design them as real as possible.

Bonus: Another Side

Thinking in Ecosystem

As Google Glass apps, good Apple Watch apps also require small pieces of information that tight to time and location, as well as light and simple interactions. So, I designed following user experience for Apple Watch at the end of this project, and I saw this part as a practice of thinking in ecosystem.

If I have more time for this side project, I would like to explor Apple Watch app a little bit more. Compared with iPhone apps, Apple Watch apps have their own interaction, usage and criteria that can tell the great from the good. How these two apps should coordinate with each other is another question I'd like to answer.
